## üß† Core Concept & Philosophy

### The Grounding Metaphor
This tool "grounds" students' understanding by:
- **Revealing WHAT** they're doing that's ineffective (behaviors)
- **Explaining WHY** they're doing it (beliefs)
- **Measuring HOW MUCH** it impacts them (scores)
- **Identifying WHERE to focus** (highest scoring subdomains)

### Disconnection from All That's Greater
When students are disconnected from the greater benevolence of life, they experience:
- **Need for total control** - Unable to trust systems, help, or natural processes
- **Isolated self-reliance** - Exhaustion from trying to do everything themselves
- **Catastrophic thinking** - Expecting disaster and making fear-based decisions
- **Self-sabotage as protection** - Creating the very disasters they fear

## üéØ Assessment Design Principles

### 1. Belief ‚Üí Behavior Separation
Each subdomain measures a specific belief AND its associated behavior as separate scores. This reveals whether:
- **Belief is driving behavior** (both high) - the belief is active and manifesting
- **Behavior exists without this belief** (behavior high, belief low) - check other subdomains for the driving belief
- **Belief exists without manifesting in this behavior** (belief high, behavior low) - manifesting through different subdomain behaviors

### 2. Clean Measurement
Behavior questions measure ONLY the behavior, without conditional phrasing like "when I feel X, I do Y." This ensures we get accurate behavioral data regardless of belief strength. Students rate behaviors as they actually occur, not theories about what drives them.

### 3. Student-Friendly Language
All subdomain names and questions use accessible language students can understand and relate to, not clinical jargon. We say "I Must Control Everything" instead of "Hypervigilant Micromanagement Pattern." Questions use everyday language about real experiences.

### 4. Compassionate Framing
Questions are framed to create self-awareness, not shame. Students are discovering patterns, not being diagnosed with disorders. Language emphasizes "noticing," "learning," and "working through" rather than failure or pathology.

### 5. Actionable Subdomains
Each subdomain is specific enough that guidance can be targeted: "Your 'Everything Will Go Wrong' pattern means..." not just "You have Fear Leading to Isolation." Specificity enables actionable next steps.

### 6. Progressive Disclosure
Students complete one subdomain at a time (one page per subdomain), preventing overwhelm. They don't see all 36 questions at once. This creates focus and reduces assessment fatigue.

### 7. Open Response Integration
Two open-ended questions per subdomain provide qualitative data that enriches analysis and makes insights specific to the individual. These questions probe the "why" behind the scores.

### 8. Scoring Transparency
Students see their scores at multiple levels (subdomain, domain, overall) with clear explanations of what each means. Transparency builds trust and understanding.

### 9. Gap Analysis Clarity
The gap between highest subdomain and domain average tells students WHERE to focus, not just that a pattern exists. This creates actionable starting points.

### 10. Non-Diagnostic Language
Throughout, we use language like "pattern," "strategy," "approach" rather than "disorder," "dysfunction," "pathology." This maintains a growth-oriented, compassionate frame.

## Domain 1: Control Leading to Isolation (CLI)

**Core Pattern:** Self-imposed suffering as primary mechanism  
**Signature:** Must personally ensure everything; refusing help, support, systems  
**Effect:** Exhaustion and scarcity through over-reliance on self and rejection of support

---

### Subdomain 1.1: "I Must Control Everything"
**Belief ‚Üí Behavior Connection:** Believing you must control everything leads to rejection of help and systems

**Belief Scale (-3 to +3):**
> "If I don't personally control every financial detail, things will fall apart"

- **-3** = Strongly agree - I'm absolutely certain everything will collapse without my total control of every detail
- **-2** = Agree - I firmly believe I must control all financial details; trusting anything else means failure
- **-1** = Slightly agree - I often feel I must control everything, though I'm questioning this need
- **+1** = Slightly disagree - I'm learning I can share control, though letting go feels dangerous
- **+2** = Disagree - I generally know shared responsibility works, though occasional control urges emerge
- **+3** = Strongly disagree - I completely trust that I don't need to control everything; collaboration and systems work

**Behavior Scale (-3 to +3):**
> "I reject systems, tools, or help because I don't trust anything I don't personally manage"

- **-3** = Always - I constantly reject all help and systems; everything must go through me personally
- **-2** = Very often - I regularly refuse systems and help, maintaining total personal control
- **-1** = Often - I frequently reject assistance and tools, unable to trust anything I don't control
- **+1** = Occasionally - I sometimes reject help, working on trusting systems and others
- **+2** = Rarely - I occasionally resist help, but generally embrace useful systems and support
- **+3** = Never - I consistently use helpful systems and accept assistance; trust in collaboration

**Feeling Scale (-3 to +3):**
> "I feel anxious and unsafe when I'm not personally managing every financial detail"

- **-3** = Always - Constant overwhelming anxiety and terror when I'm not controlling everything
- **-2** = Very often - Persistent severe anxiety about not being in complete control
- **-1** = Often - Frequent anxiety and feeling unsafe when not personally managing everything
- **+1** = Occasionally - Sometimes anxious about control, learning to trust others and systems
- **+2** = Rarely - Occasional brief anxiety, but generally comfortable with shared responsibility
- **+3** = Never - I feel completely safe with appropriate delegation; no anxiety about control

**Consequence Scale (-3 to +3):**
> "My need for control has exhausted me and prevented me from achieving financial goals"

- **-3** = Always - Complete exhaustion and massive goal failure from trying to control everything
- **-2** = Very often - Regular severe exhaustion and significant goal failure from control needs
- **-1** = Often - Frequent exhaustion and goal sabotage due to control requirements
- **+1** = Occasionally - Sometimes exhausted by control needs, working toward balance
- **+2** = Rarely - Occasional tiredness from control, but generally maintain healthy balance
- **+3** = Never - I maintain energy and achieve goals through healthy delegation and trust

**Open Response Question:**

## üìù Notes & Considerations

### Scoring Philosophy
We use **-3 to +3 scales without zero** because:
- **No neutral escape** - students must take a position one way or the other
- **Negative numbers indicate pattern IS present** - problematic/unhealthy end of spectrum
- **Positive numbers indicate pattern is NOT present** - healthy/functional end of spectrum
- **Forces engagement** - students can't hide in "neutral" and must honestly assess where they fall
- **More intuitive direction** - negative feels "bad" and positive feels "good" to most people

### Subdomain Count
**Three subdomains per domain** provides:
- **Enough granularity to be specific** - students get targeted insights, not vague generalities
- **Not so many that students get overwhelmed** - 6 total subdomains per tool is manageable
- **Clean gap analysis** - 3 is small enough to easily identify which subdomain is highest
- **Manageable GPT processing** - 6 calls per tool (one per subdomain) is efficient

### Open Response Strategy
**Two questions per subdomain** because:
- **One question might not capture the nuance** - people need multiple angles to explore
- **Two allows exploration and confirmation** - first question opens, second deepens
- **More than two risks overwhelming students** - assessment fatigue is real
- **Provides rich qualitative data for GPT** - enough context without overwhelming

### Gap Thresholds
The gap classification helps students understand where to focus:

- **< 5 points = DIFFUSE** (all subdomains similar, work on domain as whole)
  - No single subdomain stands out significantly
  - Address the overall domain pattern
  
- **5-15 points = FOCUSED** (one subdomain elevated, start there)
  - One subdomain is moderately higher than average
  - Start with highest but recognize others contribute
  
- **> 15 points = HIGHLY FOCUSED** (one subdomain dominant, clear starting point)
  - One subdomain is dramatically higher
  - Very clear where to begin work

**These thresholds are based on:**
- **Practical significance** - 5 points = noticeable difference students can feel
- **Statistical variation** - avoiding over-interpretation of small, random gaps
- **Actionability** - students need clear guidance, not ambiguity about next steps
